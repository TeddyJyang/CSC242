**The Last Hot-N-Ready**

The rain came down in sheets the night Marco lost his job. He sat in his car outside the strip mall, watching the neon sign buzz and flicker through the wet windshield — that cartoon Roman emperor grinning back at him, toga and all, holding his pizza like a scepter.

He had exactly eleven dollars in his pocket.

Inside, the place smelled the way it always had — that particular alchemy of warm dough and oregano that Marco associated with Friday nights as a kid, when his dad would come home with the flat orange box tucked under his arm like he'd slain something.

"Hot-N-Ready?" the teenager behind the counter asked without looking up.

"Yeah," Marco said. "The classic."

Six dollars and change. He stood there holding the box in both hands like an offering while the rain hammered the roof.

He ate in the car. The pizza was what it was — not extraordinary, not trying to be. The cheese pulled in long, forgiving strings. The crust had that familiar chew to it, soft in the center, a little crisp underneath. No pretense. No forty-five minute wait. No reservation.

By the third slice, something in his chest had loosened.

He thought about his dad, who'd always said *it's not about the pizza, it's about what happens around the pizza.* But tonight Marco was alone, and the pizza still worked. That felt important, somehow. A thing that showed up for you even when nothing else did.

He drove home with four slices left in the box and the emperor still grinning on the seat beside him, unbothered, eternal.

*Pizza! Pizza!*
